~ Ghost Notes ~

A machine plays the same hit at the same volume, forever. A human does not. Ghost notes are the whispered snares — the little taps between the loud ones — that make a drum pattern breathe like a person is playing it. Click cells on the snare lane to add them. Listen for the groove waking up.

Why Little Hits Make Big Songs

1. Dynamics are half the conversation. When you listen to a great drummer, you're not just hearing which notes they play — you're hearing how hard. A kick pattern where every hit is 80% velocity is a robot. The same pattern with one hit at 100% and another at 40% is a person telling you a story. Ghost notes are the quietest hits, and they do the most work.

2. Ghosts give the main hits their weight. Try this: play with only the main snare on 2 and 4, no ghosts. It sounds clean, but a little stiff. Now add some ghosts on the sixteenths between. The main snares get louder in your ear — not in absolute dB, but in contrast. That's the whole trick. A loud note only feels loud next to a soft one.

3. The J Dilla preset is not a random pattern. Dilla built grooves by putting ghost notes in places that felt wrong on paper — unexpected sixteenths, hits pushed and pulled together with microtiming. The whole "neo-soul" feel is the collision of these two lessons: pocket plus ghosts. Load the Dilla preset, go back to 02-the-pocket.html, imagine the snare pulled 15ms late. That's the whole genre.

4. Hat density is the other speed dial. The same kick-and-snare pattern under quarters feels open and patient. Under 8ths, it's rock. Under 16ths, it's drum & bass or boom-bap. You haven't changed the pattern — you've changed how busy the air is between the hits. Try it.

The pattern you build here is yours. The presets are just starting points. When you stop thinking of a groove as "four quarter-notes a bar" and start hearing it as "a density of tiny decisions," you've crossed a threshold.

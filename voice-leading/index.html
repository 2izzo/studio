Voice Leading

Same four notes, a hundred different voicings. Voice leading is the craft of choosing — at every chord change — the voicing that moves the fewest notes the shortest distance. Do it well and arrangements sound smooth, composed, professional. Do it badly and every chord change sounds like a jump cut.

Prereq (optional): Module 1 — Chord Progressions gives you the chord vocabulary these lessons move between.

Glossary

Voice leading
The craft of moving from one chord to the next by making each voice (note) travel as little as possible. The difference between a choir sounding like a choir and sounding like four soloists hitting chords together.
Common tone
A note that appears in both of two consecutive chords. A C is common to both C and Am, so it can hold (stay put) across the change.
Semitone
The smallest interval in Western music — one fret on a guitar, one key on a piano (black or white). The unit we measure voice motion in. C → C# = 1 semitone.
Inversion
A voicing where a non-root note is in the bass. C/E means a C major chord with E on the bottom (first inversion). Inversions exist mostly to make voice leading smoother.
Root position
A voicing where the chord's root is the lowest note. C E G for C major. Easy to play, easy to teach, usually the worst choice for voice leading.
ii–V–I
The most common three-chord progression in Western music, especially jazz. In C: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7. Its power comes almost entirely from how beautifully the voice leading works when you voice it right.
Circle of fifths
A loop through all 12 roots where each chord's root is a fifth below the previous. Progressions that follow the circle voice-lead themselves — that's why they feel "inevitable."
Greedy algorithm
A strategy that makes the locally best choice at every step, without looking ahead. Voice leading is one of the rare musical problems where greedy actually works — pick the smoothest move each time, and the whole progression takes care of itself.
Parallel motion
When two voices move the same direction by the same interval. Parallel fifths and octaves are the famous counter-example in classical voice leading — they make the voices feel like they've merged. Avoided traditionally, used on purpose in rock and metal (power chords).
Contrary motion
When two voices move in opposite directions — one up while the other goes down. The sound of independence between voices, and the classical fix for parallel-motion problems.

Glossary

Progression
A sequence of chords that pulls your ear through a key. "I–V–vi–IV" is a progression; so is every song you've ever hummed.
Roman numeral
Chord-by-function label inside a key. In C major, I = C, V = G, vi = Am. Uppercase = major, lowercase = minor. Lets you talk about a progression without caring what key you're in.
Diatonic
"Inside the key." The seven chords built only from the notes of a given major scale. C major's diatonic chords: C Dm Em F G Am Bdim. Your starting vocabulary.
Seventh chord
A triad plus the note a 7th above the root. Adds color — jazzier, softer, more "adult" than plain triads. Cmaj7, Dm7, G7. The sound of the Lofi Lab.
ii–V–I
The workhorse of Western harmony. ii → V → I = tension building, dominant pulling, home resolving. Jazz standards, pop bridges, movie endings — all run on this.
I–V–vi–IV
The pop progression. Hundreds of hit songs, one loop. Bright, emotive, loops forever. Get this one in your body before Module 2.
Secondary dominant
A V chord borrowed from a different key, used to point at a chord that isn't the tonic. V/V in C major = D7, which pulls to G. One chord, one moment of extra gravity.
Modal interchange
Borrowing a chord from a parallel mode. In C major, swapping F (IV) for Fm (iv from C minor). One chord, a big mood shift. Covered in depth in Module 2.
Passing chord
A chord that isn't part of the progression but slides between two chords that are, to smooth the movement. Usually a diminished or a secondary dominant. The filler that makes the line feel deliberate.

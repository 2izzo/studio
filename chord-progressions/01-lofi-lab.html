The Math (why it works)



  
1. ii–V–I is gravity. Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7 is the most famous cadence in jazz. Each chord root moves down a fifth — that's walking counterclockwise around the circle of fifths, the most resolved direction there is. Your ear hears "tension → release → home."


  
2. The 7ths soften everything. A plain C triad (C-E-G) is bright and declarative. Cmaj7 adds a B — a half-step under C — which creates a dreamy, unresolved shimmer. Dm7 adds a C; G7 adds an F. Every chord has one note that wants to move somewhere, so the loop never feels static.


  
3. Am7 is the "sigh." We don't stop on the I — we fall to vi (Am7), the relative minor. Same notes as C major, but melancholy. This is the "lofi turnaround" — it keeps the loop from resolving too hard, so it can repeat forever without feeling repetitive.


  
4. The vinyl dust isn't decoration — it's context. Crackle + lowpass filter + slight pitch wobble tells your brain "this is old, imperfect, human." That imperfection signal is half the genre. Strip it out and the same chords sound like a smooth-jazz commercial.


  
Try dragging the lowpass down to ~900 Hz and the crackle up. Watch the genre collapse into "rainy Tokyo apartment, 3am."

~ Call & Response ~

Good melodies have a conversation with themselves. The antecedent asks a question; the consequent answers. Where you land the last note of the answer is what decides whether the phrase feels closed, open, or hanging. Pick a question. Hear three answers.

● Resolved
Lands on tonic (1)
Full stop. The ear settles; the phrase is complete. This is how nursery rhymes and pop hooks end.
● Open
Lands on dominant (5)
A comma, not a period. The phrase feels paused mid-thought. Perfect for setting up a second line.
● Cliffhanger
Lands on leading tone (7)
One half-step below home. The tension is almost physical — you can feel the note wanting to resolve.

The idea

A phrase is a complete musical thought. Two-bar phrases pair up into four-bar sentences the way question pairs with answer. This is the period, the oldest form in Western tonal music — Mozart, Lennon, and Taylor Swift all use it.

The three endings above are the three classical cadences. The authentic cadence (ending on 1) is full punctuation. The half cadence (ending on 5) is a comma — it says "I'm not done yet." The ending on 7 (the leading tone) doesn't have a classical name on its own because it's refusing to cadence — a cliffhanger demanding continuation.

Listen to Twinkle Twinkle Little Star: "twinkle twinkle little star" ends on 5 (open). "How I wonder what you are" ends on 1 (closed). That's the whole game. Great songwriters just do it with better tunes.

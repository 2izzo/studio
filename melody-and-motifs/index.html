Glossary

Motif
A short musical idea — usually 2 to 6 notes — that's distinctive enough to recognize when it comes back. The DNA of a melody. Beethoven's 5th is one four-note motif for twenty minutes.
Transpose
Move every note of a motif up or down by the same interval. Shape preserved; pitch center moved. The most common transformation in tonal music.
Invert
Flip every interval upside-down around a fixed anchor note. What went up now goes down. Bach's fugues live on this move.
Retrograde
Play the motif backwards — last note first, first note last. Rare in common-practice tonal music, central in 12-tone and film scoring.
Sequence
State a motif, then immediately restate it at a new pitch level. The engine of Baroque melody and still one of the most satisfying moves in pop writing.
Antecedent / Consequent
The two halves of a musical sentence. The antecedent sets up a question; the consequent answers it. Together they make a period.
Cadence
How a phrase ends. Landing on the tonic = authentic cadence (closed). Landing on the dominant = half cadence (open, like a comma). Landing elsewhere = unresolved.
Chord tone
A note that belongs to the chord currently playing. Root, 3rd, 5th, 7th of Cmaj7 are C E G B. Chord tones on strong beats are the skeleton of a melody.
Passing tone
A note that bridges two chord tones by stepping through an in-between pitch. Almost always on a weak beat. The connective tissue of melodic writing.
Chromatic approach
A note outside the key that resolves by half-step to a chord tone. Huge part of what makes jazz and sophisticated pop sound the way they do.

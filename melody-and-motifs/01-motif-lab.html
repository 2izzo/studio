The idea

Melody lives by repetition with variation. A listener needs to hear something enough times to recognize it, but not so many times that they stop listening. The transformations above are how composers thread that needle: same idea, different clothes.

Transpose moves the whole shape up or down — Beethoven's "da-da-da-DUM" appears at dozens of pitch levels in the first movement. Invert turns a rising figure into a falling one; Bach loved this. Retrograde reads the motif backwards — rarer, but Stravinsky and Schoenberg used it constantly. Sequence is just transpose-and-append, the single most-used move in Western melody.

Try this: start with Mary Had a Little Lamb. Hit invert. That "little lamb" shape now falls where it used to rise. Hit it again — and you've basically written a different folk song. The shape is preserved; the world inside the shape is different.

Glossary

Mode
A rotation of a scale starting on a different degree. C major starting on D = D Dorian. Same notes, different tonal center, different character.
Drone
A sustained note that establishes the tonal center. Everything you play on top of a drone gets heard relative to it. The drone is what makes a mode sound like a mode.
Characteristic note
The one note that most distinguishes a mode from its closest neighbor. Dorian's is the ♮6 (natural 6th over a minor chord). Lydian's is the ♯4.
Ionian
The "default" major scale. C-D-E-F-G-A-B. Bright, resolved, the sound of "Happy Birthday." Mode 1.
Dorian
Minor with a raised 6th. D-E-F-G-A-B-C. Smooth, jazzy, not quite sad — the "Billie Jean" vibe. Mode 2.
Phrygian
Minor with a flat 2nd. E-F-G-A-B-C-D. Dark, Spanish, mysterious — instant flamenco. Mode 3.
Lydian
Major with a raised 4th. F-G-A-B-C-D-E. Dreamy, floating, sci-fi. The Simpsons theme. Mode 4.
Mixolydian
Major with a flat 7th. G-A-B-C-D-E-F. Bluesy, rock, confident — "Sweet Home Alabama." Mode 5.
Aeolian
The natural minor scale. A-B-C-D-E-F-G. Melancholy, dark, introspective. Mode 6.
Locrian
Diminished — flat 2nd and flat 5th. B-C-D-E-F-G-A. Unstable, tense, rarely used as a key center. Mode 7.
Modal interchange
Borrowing a chord from a parallel mode. In C major, using Fm (the iv from C minor) instead of F (the IV). One chord, massive mood shift.
Parent scale
The major scale that contains all seven modes. C major is the parent of D Dorian, E Phrygian, F Lydian, G Mixolydian, A Aeolian, and B Locrian. Same notes, seven perspectives.

Why This Works

1. Major and minor are parallel universes. C major has the notes C D E F G A B. C minor has C D E♭ F G A♭ B♭. Three notes are different. When you "borrow" a chord from C minor, you're smuggling one of those altered notes into a major context — a brief glimpse of the parallel universe.

2. One note is enough. The difference between F major (IV) and F minor (iv) is a single note: A becomes A♭. That's it. But that half-step drops the whole emotional temperature of the progression. Your ear was expecting brightness; it got a shadow. The contrast is what makes it hit.

3. This is everywhere. "Creep" by Radiohead: the III chord (E major in a G-minor context, but the effect is borrowed). "Let It Be": the ♭VII. "Space Oddity" by Bowie: iv in a major key. Every songwriter who's ever written a chord that makes you go "oh, that one" — they probably borrowed it.

Try toggling iv on and off during playback. Listen for the exact beat where the mood drops — that's the A♭ landing. One note. One universe.

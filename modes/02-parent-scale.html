The Illusion

1. There are no "mode notes." C Ionian, D Dorian, E Phrygian, F Lydian, G Mixolydian, A Aeolian, B Locrian — every single one uses the notes C D E F G A B. Not one note changes. The only thing that moves is the drone.

2. The drone is the trick. Your ear interprets every note relative to the drone. When the drone is on C, the note F is a perfect 4th — stable, normal. When the drone moves to F, that same note F is now the root — and B (the same B that was there all along) becomes a ♯4, the "floating" Lydian interval. Nothing moved. Your frame moved.

3. This is why modes feel different. The intervals between consecutive notes in C major aren't evenly spaced — there are whole steps and half steps. When you rotate the starting point, the half steps fall in different places relative to the root. That's the whole game. Dorian has its half steps between 2–3 and 6–7. Phrygian has them between 1–2 and 5–6. Same raw material, different internal geometry.

Try this: start with drone on C (Ionian). Hit "play scale ascending." Now move the drone to D without stopping. Hear the scale again in your head — it's the same notes, but your brain re-parses them around D. That re-parsing is the illusion, and it's the entire foundation of modal music.
